Aluminum Hip Belt Buckle

A simple-to-manufacture hard-wearing hip belt buckle to replace plastic junk.

Table of Contents

  • Objective
  • Design Overview
  • Manufacturing
  • Usage
  • Improvements

Background

A few years ago, the hip belt buckle on my girlfriend's hiking bag broke. I had just bought a 3d printer, and was having loads of fun learning to design things in FreeCAD. I figured I could iterative design my way to the perfect buckle in no time! I was wrong about how long it would take, and it's not perfect, but it was fun.

Objective

The buckle needed to achieve several things.

  1. The buckle must not be plastic, so that it can be properly recycled if/when it breaks.
  2. The buckle must maintain a stable connection even in the absence of tension across the buckle.
  3. The buckle should be composed of as few parts as possible.
  4. The buckle should be easy to use, like any other buckle.
  5. Creating the buckle must not cause me to buy additional tools.

Design Overview

Given requirement (1.) and the other kinds of projects I was working on at the time, I quickly decided that the best approach for me would be to design something which would be composed of discrete sheet metal parts.

In pursuit of requirement (3.), I scrapped the idea of creating a 3 dimensional assembly by stacking sheet metal layers. This would be expensive, and I was mostly interested in approaching this as a problem to be solved by novel geometry.

Requirement (2.) suggests to me that the movement which the user executes to combine the two halves of the buckle should NOT be in or opposite the direction of tension, but along some other vector entirely. With this in mind, I decided the two halves of the buckle should rotate against each other to combine.

The design which arose out of these ideas is two basic shapes cut out of sheet metal. One half remains a flat sheet which is approximately a circle, and the other half is approximately a Y shape which has three prongs that get bent up to capture the edges of the flat circular half and hold it in place. As the prongs are not capable of moving, a particular pattern of slots is cut into the flat circular half which allows the circular half to be captured by and removed from the prongs through a translation and rotation.

To prevent the buckle coming apart accidentally, the three prongs are bent slightly narrower than the thickness of the flat circular piece. This causes them to act as springs when the flat half is inserted. Through this slight springing, a downward pressure is applied from the prongs onto the flat half, providing friction which resists accidental rotation. Additionally, the points of the prongs settle into small grooves in the flat circular piece, making the the locked position one of stability, requiring intentional energy input to rotate the buckle out of the locked state.

Manufacturing

I used .040" (1.016mm) 5052 aluminum for this project since it lends itself so well to bending. The aluminum was all laser cut by SendCutSend, and bent into shape by me. The hand tools which are used to bend the sheet metal are all of a custom design, and are composed of layers which are bolted together. The assemblies are part 3d printed, part sheet metal for reinforcement.

To use the hand tool, a prong is set into the neck of the tool, passing under the long bolt which acts as the pivot point and fixes the inside radius of the bend. The two halves of the tool are then brought together, and the prong is forced to bend around the center bolt. Each of the three prongs can be bent on the same tool, and there are locating tabs on the tool to keep the sheet metal in position as it is being bent.

Unit Cost @ Qt. $4.04 $1.59 $0.98

So in materials the buckle could cost anywhere from $4.04 to $0.98 per unit. I am guessing this lower cost is at least 10x what it costs to manufacture a plastic hip belt buckle, but that wasn't the point of this.

Usage

I think the buckle achieves requirement (4.) of being easy to use. It doesn't have the same one-handed quick-release ability as a typical hip belt buckle, but putting it together and taking it apart is one fluid motion that just as natural as any other hip belt buckle after a few minutes wearing it.

Putting it together is 4 steps.

  1. Bring the flat piece over the bent piece, such that the single prong falls into the slot carved into the middle of the flat piece.
  2. Angle both pieces so the slot in the flat piece is in line with the prong which is now inside it.
  3. Slide the two halves together until the prong hits reaches the end of the slot.
  4. Rotate the two halves so the prong moves to the end of the curved slot, and the two side prongs come to rest in the small slots carved into the flat piece.

Improvements

Like most things I made, this will never truly be done for me. The remaining element which I don't feel satisfied with is my ladder-lock solution. I don't feel it has the same smooth action as a normal buckle when I am tightening/loosening the webbing on the hip belt. That being said, I've been using the buckle on my hiking bag for close to a year now, and in a practical sense it works just fine.
