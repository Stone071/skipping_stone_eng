Audio Bandpass Filter

Revising a three band audio equalizer from class

Table of Contents

  • Background
  • Requirements
  • Design
  • Appendix A: Solutions for Transfer Functions and System Characteristics

Background

A lab project we performed early in undergrad was implementing a 3 band audio equalizer. We were advised to use a multiple-feedback bandpass filter for at least one stage of the amplifier, and given specifications in the form of center frequency, quality factor, and gain for each channel.

That lab flew by, and I was so floored when the equalizer worked that I didn't stop to question some aspects of its performance which could have been much better. Here, I'll be taking a closer look at the most interesting portion of the equalizer which we designed, which is the bandpass filter. I'll be revising the design in favor of cascaded high-pass and low-pass filters, and making it clear why.

Requirements

The Given Specifications

Center Frequency Quality Factor Gain
Bass 200 Hz +- 10% 0.7 +- 10% 0.4 - 8
Mid 1100 Hz +- 10% 0.7 +- 10% 0.2 - 4
Treble 6000 Hz +- 10% 0.7 +- 10% 0.1 - 2

The center frequency and gain are intuitive requirements. Each channel must target a specific passband of frequencies, and the gain needs to be adjustable so the user can notice the difference. The quality factor given was not immediately intuitive. Quality factor is a measure how much a system damps oscillations. A system with a low quality factor (Q < 1/2) is overdamped, and will not oscillate. A system with a high quality factor (Q > 1/2) is underdamped, and will oscillate when excited by a specific frequency, and will continue oscillating longer depending on how high the quality factor is. A system Quality factor Q = 1/2 is critically damped, and will not oscillate, but also remains quickly responsive to sharp inputs. In a bandpass filter, quality factor has an effect on bandwidth as well, with high quality factors producing a more narrowband, peaked response. By selecting 0.7 as the quality factor, we were attempting to produce a flatter response in the passband, and not have a strong peak at the center frequency.

The Missed Requirement

When designing for audio and many other applications, it is desirable to amplify selected frequencies evenly across the entire bandwidth. You effectively want to take a rectangular window of frequencies and boost them all the same amount. Unfortunately, this is not what was achieved with the equalizer designed in lab. To the right is a frequency response plot from the output of the 3 channel equalizer.

It is very clear in this frequency response that the frequencies in between the center frequency of each bandpass filter are not being effectively amplified. The amplitude which is achieved at center frequency is not being achieved throughout the passband of each filter. There are a couple things contributing to this. Firstly, the bandpass topology used here is not the best choice. Multiple-feedback bandpass filters are good for high Q, low bandwidth applications, selectively boosting a very narrow frequency band. Additionally, the slope at which the MFB attenuates frequencies farther away from its center frequency is quite shallow, at -20dB/dec. This is low in comparison to a typical second-order filter, which would typically show a -40dB/dec slope. This shallow slope causes the filters responses near each other in the frequency spectrum to overlap. Together, these characteristics make the MFB topology a poor choice for this bandpass application.

Below, the shallow -20dB/dec slope and prominent peak of the second order multiple-feedback bandpass filter can be seen. The actual transfer function for the filter as implemented is

\(\frac{-sCR_{2}}{C^{2}R_{1}R_{2}s^{2} + 2CR_{1}s + 1} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}}\)

The solution for this equation be found in Appendix A.

Design

Old

The old design was a second order multiple-feedback bandpass filter. As discussed above, it cannot achieve the flat response across the passband which we desire.

New

A very simple solution for creating a passband of any width while having a flat response in the middle frequencies is to cascade a high-pass and a low-pass filter together. The high-pass removes low frequencies, the low-pass removes high frequencies, and we are left with a window of unchanged response in the middle. This concept is illustrated simply by these three plots below.

One can see how overlaying the first two plots creates the third, where there is a flat passband between 300Hz and 3kHz. Additionally, the slope on either side of the passband is seen to be -40dB/dec, which makes it easier to accurately target frequencies without overlapping responses with adjacent filters.

Circuit Layout

Topology

The topology being employed here can be understood with some intuition. In the lowpass filter, placing a capacitor in the feedback path causes the impedance seen by the feedback to be reduced as frequency increases, so feedback increases with frequency and reduces gain. Additionally, there is a capacitor shunt to ground at the noninverting input. This capacitor presents a low impedance path to ground at high frequency, causing high frequency inputs to be sent to ground rather than the input. Through these two elements, both the input signal and gain can be reduced with frequency, causing the -40db/dec slope which is characteristic of second-order filters. For the highpass filter, the two capacitors along the input path present very high impedance to low-frequency inputs, effectively blocking them. Once again, having two frequency dependent elements affecting the input-output relationship creates the second order response.

Design Equations

Sallen-Key Low-Pass Filter

The low-pass filter has a transfer function of

\( \frac{\frac{1}{C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}}}{s^{2} + 2(\frac{C_{4}(R_{1}+R_{2})}{2\sqrt{C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}}})(\frac{1}{\sqrt{C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}}}) + \frac{1}{C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}}} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \)

The solution for this equation can be found in Appendix A.

From the transfer function, we can define expressions for important characteristics like quality factor and natural frequency.

\( \frac{1}{\sqrt{C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}}} = \omega_{n}\)

\( \frac{\sqrt{C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}}}{C_{4}(R_{1}+R_{2})} = Q\)

The natural frequency and quality factor together characterize the region where the passband begins to turn down and get reduced by the filter. The natural frequency is generally the frequency at which the passband transitions into frequencies being blocked by the filter. The quality factor determines how close to the natural frequency the passband comes before turning down, and also the performance of the filter nearing this edge. High quality factors come very close to the natural frequency before turning down, but also experience a peak in the amplitude response near this edge. Lower quality factors will not approach the natural frequency as closely before turning down, but experience a flat passband. A butterworth design is one which has a maximally flat passband while approaching the natural frequency as closely as possible, and is achieved by specifying

\( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = Q\)

Given that we have two characterstics which we would like to design for and four independent values to specify in the Sallen-Key topology, common practice is to constrain the four independents by specifying a relationship between the two resistors and the two capacitors.

\( R_{1} = mR \)

\( R_{2} = \frac{1}{m}R \)

\( C_{3} = nC \)

\( C_{4} = \frac{1}{n}C \)

Now, with these relationships defined, there are still four independent variables, m, R, n, and C, but the expressions for quality factor and natural frequency no longer depend on the same subset of independent variables.

\( \frac{nm}{m^{2}+1} = Q \)

\( \frac{1}{RC} = \omega_{n} \)

Now, by selecting \( m = 1 \) and \( n = \sqrt{2} \), we can set Q for butterworth or maximally flat response. By choosing values of R and C, we can set the natural frequency wherever we want frequencies to begin getting blocked without affecting the quality factor.

Sallen-Key High-Pass Filter

The high-pass filter has a transfer function of

\( \frac{s^{2}}{s^{2} + 2(\frac{(C_{1}+C_{2})R3}{2\sqrt{R_{3}R_{4}C_{1}C_{2}}})(\frac{1}{\sqrt{R_{3}R_{4}C_{1}C_{2}}}) + \frac{1}{R_{3}R_{4}C_{1}C_{2}}} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}}\)

The solution for this equation can be found in Appendix A.

Using the same component relationships described above, we can express quality factor and natural frequency in terms of R and C.

\( \frac{n}{m(n^{2}+1)} = Q \)

\( \frac{1}{RC} = \omega_{n} \)

In this case, to achieve butterworth response, \( m = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \) and \( n = 1 \). Then once again values of R and C must be chosen around the desired cut-off frequency.

Simulation

The simulation shows the desired outcome on each channel, with a flat passband fenced between two slopes which are steep enough to not interact with neighboring filters in disfunctional ways.

Above is the amplitude frequency response for the three channels, with the low channel in red, the middle channel in green, and the high channel in orange.

Above is the amplitude frequency response of the output (blue) shown in comparison to the amplitude frequency response for each channel.

It can clearly be seen that the amplitude frequency response for the final output is not completely flat. This is because the edges of the frequency spectrum for each filter do overlap and interact. In fact, frequencies targeted by the middle channel were specifically selected to overlap with the low and high channels such that there are not major gaps in the frequency response, which would result in some frequencies between each channel getting tuned out. As a result of this, there are some secondary peaks in the frequency response where the middle channel meets with the low and high channels. These are likely to not be significantly audible, and are quickly overshadowed once the gain on any individual channel is turned up above the other channels.

Appendix A: Solutions for Transfer Functions and System Characteristics

Multiple Feedback Bandpass Filter

Node Voltage Method

Node 1, between R1 and C

\(\frac{V_{1} - V_{in}}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{1} - V_{out}}{\frac{1}{C s}} + \frac{V_{1}}{\frac{1}{C s}} = 0\)

\(\frac{V_{1}}{R_{1}} - \frac{V_{in}}{R_{1}} + C V_{1} s + - C V_{out} s + C V_{1} s = 0\)

Node 2, at the inverting input

\(- \frac{V_{out}}{R_{2}} - \frac{V_{1}}{\frac{1}{Cs}} = 0\)

\(- \frac{V_{out}}{R_{2}} - C V_{1} s = 0\)

\( - \frac{V_{out}}{C R_{2} s} = V_{1}\)

Substituting Node 2 equation into Node 1 equation

\(-\frac{\frac{V_{out}}{CR_{2}s}}{R_{1}} - \frac{V_{in}}{R_{1}} - \frac{CV_{out}s}{CR_{2}s} - CV_{out}s - \frac{CV_{out}s}{CR_{2}s} = 0\)

\(\frac{-V_{out}}{C s R_{1} R_{2}} - \frac{V_{in}}{R_{1}} - \frac{2V_{out}}{R_{2}} - CV_{out}s = 0\)

\(- V_{out}\left( \frac{1}{C s R_{1} R_{2}} + \frac{2}{R_{2}} + Cs \right) = \frac{V_{in}}{R_{1}}\)

\(- V_{out} \left( \frac{R_{1}}{C s R_{1} R_{2}} + \frac{2 R_{1}}{R_{2}} + CR_{1}s\right) = V_{in}\)

\(- V_{out} \left(\frac{1}{C R_{2} s} + \frac{C 2 R_{1} s}{C R_{2} s} + \frac{C^{2} s^{2} R_{1} R_{2}}{C R_{2} s}\right) = V_{in}\)

Final Transfer Function

\(\frac{-sCR_{2}}{C^{2}R_{1}R_{2}s^{2} + 2CR_{1}s + 1} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}}\)

2nd order transfer function standard form

\(\frac{K \omega_{n}^{2}}{s^{2} + 2\zeta\omega_{n}s + \omega_{n}^{2}} = H\)

Placing the transfer function in the standard form, we can relate the component values to the characteristics of the second order system.

Fundamental Frequency

\(\frac{1}{C \sqrt{R_{1} R_{2}}} = \omega_{n}\)

Damping Ratio

\(\frac{\sqrt{R_{1} R_{2}}}{R_{2}} = \zeta\)

Sallen-Key Low Pass Filter

(1) KCL at Noninverting Input

\( \frac{V_{1}}{Z_{C4}} + \frac{V_{1}-V_{2}}{R_{2}} = 0 \)

\( \frac{V_{out}}{Z_{C4}} + \frac{V_{out}-V_{2}}{R_{2}} = 0 \)

\( R_{2}(\frac{V_{out}}{Z_{C4}}+\frac{V_{out}}{R_{2}}) = V_{2} \)

\( V_{out}(\frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}}+1) = V_{2} \)

(2) KCL at feedback node

\( \frac{V_{2}-V_{1}}{R_{2}} + \frac{V_{2}-V_{in}}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{2}-V_{out}}{Z_{C3}} + = 0 \)

\( \frac{V_{2}-V_{out}}{R_{2}} + \frac{V_{2}-V_{in}}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{2}-V_{out}}{Z_{C3}} + = 0 \)

Substitute (1) into (2)

\( \frac{V_{out}(\frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}})}{R_{2}} + \frac{V_{out}(\frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}}+1)-V_{in}}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{out}(\frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}})}{Z_{C3}} = 0 \)

\( \frac{V_{out}}{Z_{C4}} + \frac{V_{out}(\frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}})}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{out}}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{in}}{R_{1}} + \frac{V_{out}(\frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}})}{Z_{C3}} = 0 \)

\( V_{out}(\frac{1}{Z_{C4}} + \frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}R_{1}} + \frac{1}{R_{1}} + \frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C3}Z_{C4}}) = \frac{V_{in}}{R_{1}} \)

\( R_{1}(\frac{1}{Z_{C4}} + \frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}R_{1}} + \frac{1}{R_{1}} + \frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C3}Z_{C4}}) = \frac{V_{in}}{V_{out}} \)

\( \frac{R_{1}}{Z_{C4}} + \frac{R_{2}}{Z_{C4}} + 1 + \frac{R_{2}R_{1}}{Z_{C3}Z_{C4}} = \frac{V_{in}}{V_{out}} \)

\( \frac{R_{1}Z_{C3}+R_{2}Z_{C3}+Z_{C3}Z_{C4}+R_{2}R_{1}}{Z_{C4}Z_{C4}} = \frac{V_{in}}{V_{out}} \)

\( \frac{Z_{C4}Z_{C4}}{R_{1}Z_{C3}+R_{2}Z_{C3}+Z_{C3}Z_{C4}+R_{2}R_{1}} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \)

\( \frac{(\frac{1}{sC_{3}})(\frac{1}{sC_{4}})}{R_{1}R_{2}+\frac{1}{sC_{3}}(R_{2}+R_{1})+(\frac{1}{sC_{3}})(\frac{1}{sC_{4}})} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \)

\( \frac{\frac{1}{s^{2}C_{3}C_{4}}}{R_{1}R_{2}+\frac{1}{sC_{3}}(R_{2}+R_{1})+\frac{1}{s^{2}C_{3}C_{4}}} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \)

\( \frac{\frac{1}{s^{2}C_{3}C_{4}}}{\frac{s^{2}C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2}+sR_{2}C_{4}+sR_{1}C_{4}+1}{s^{2}C_{3}C_{4}}} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \)

\( \frac{1}{(C_{3}C_{4}R_{1}R_{2})s^{2}+(R_{2}C_{4}+R_{1}C_{4})s+1} = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} \)

Transfer Function to Standard Form

Sallen-Key High Pass Filter

Beginning from the transfer function for Sallen-Key topology generalized for four impedances.

\( \frac{Z_{3}Z_{4}}{Z_{1}Z_{2}+Z_{3}(Z_{2}+Z_{1})+Z_{3}Z_{4}} = \frac{V_{in}}{V_{out}} \)

\( \frac{R_{3}R_{4}}{Z_{1}Z_{2}+R_{3}(Z_{2}+Z_{1})+R_{3}R_{4}} = \frac{V_{in}}{V_{out}} \)

\( \frac{\frac{s^{2}R_{3}R_{4}C_{1}C_{2}}{s^{2}C_{1}C_{2}}}{\frac{1 + sC_{1}R_{3} + sC_{2}R_{3} + s^{2}C_{1}C_{2}R_{3}R_{4}}{s^{2}C_{1}C_{2}}} = \frac{V_{in}}{V_{out}} \)
